## Pillar 1 — "Watch It Grow" (Time-lapses)

### P1-A: Trunk-to-Canopy — Ficus `[NOW]`
A single Ficus starts as a bare, stubby trunk in a shallow training pot and the camera locks into cinematic orbit while seasons blur past. Leaves flush in waves, thicken, and catch the light differently as the branches extend; the bark darkens and the nebari begins to flare at the soil line. The whole arc — zero leaves to a dense, full canopy — compresses into under a minute and ends with a slow pull-back to reveal the finished silhouette. This is the "hook" video: beautiful, wordless, instantly shareable.

### P1-B: Trunk-to-Canopy — Juniper `[NOW]`
Same format as the Ficus video but cut to feel quieter and more architectural. Juniper grows slowly and at sharp angles, so the time-lapse communicates *patience* rather than explosion — the camera lingers longer between cuts. The muted blue-green foliage and angular branching make the species feel ancient even at small scale. Ends on a tight shot of a single jin or deadwood stub catching the light.

### P1-C: Trunk-to-Canopy — Japanese Maple `[NOW]`
The Maple's opposite-bud branching pattern produces a perfect, symmetrical fan that no other species matches — this video leads with that. Spring flush is the visual climax: the camera is tight on the canopy as leaves emerge in a wave of bright green, then widens to show the full form. If the seasonal palette includes autumn color, hold on that moment longest. This is the most "botanical art" of the three trunk-to-canopy videos.

### P1-D: Species Side-by-Side — Juniper vs Maple `[NOW]`
Split screen, same starting conditions, same number of seasons. Left side: Juniper, slow and angular, building structure one careful branch at a time. Right side: Maple, fast and symmetrical, the canopy filling in almost impatiently. No narration needed — the difference in branching architecture (alternate vs. opposite buds) tells the story. Text overlay at the end: species name, years elapsed, one-line flavor.

### P1-E: Root Development Time-lapse `[NOW]`
Camera angle drops low — at or below soil level — looking up at the base of the trunk while seasons tick forward. The roots thicken, splay outward, and begin to grip the soil. This is specifically about nebari development and is the one video targeted squarely at bonsai hobbyists who know what surface roots mean for a tree's visual quality. Text at the end: *"Nebari. The roots that make a bonsai look ancient."*

### P1-F: "We Improved the Trunk" — Before/After `[LATER: requires vertex jitter + junction swell geometry]`
A split-screen or cut between the same species and age class before and after the trunk geometry pass. Left is the old smooth cylinder; right shows vertex noise, taper variation, and junction swell. No need for narration — the geometry does all the talking. This is a dev-log video that serves double duty as a general "look how far we've come" re-engagement post for existing followers.

### P1-G: Bud Break Close-Up `[LATER: requires bud visuals]`
Extreme close-up on a single branch from late winter through early spring. The bud swells over several frames, the tip cracks open, and the first furled leaf emerges and unfurls. This 20–30 second clip has no equivalent in any other bonsai game and will travel far with the enthusiast community. Record it at very low cinematic speed so the motion feels almost geological.

## Pillar 2 — "The Craft" (System Explainers)

Each video follows the same 4-beat structure: **Problem (5s) → Mechanic (20–30s) → Consequence (10s) → CTA (5s)**

For each video, a companion *Game Instructions* file will be created with exact, repeatable player steps for consistent recording and play-testing.

### P2-A: "How Wiring Works" `[NOW]`
Most bonsai games treat wiring as a stat toggle — BONSAIMANCER makes it tactile and permanent. This video walks through selecting a branch, applying wire in the correct direction, bending the branch to its target angle, and then intentionally leaving the wire on too long while the seasons fast-forward. The wire bite is the payoff: the bark swells around the coils and the scar stays in the mesh forever, exactly as it does on a real tree. Ends on the scar as a reminder that every decision in this game has a physical record.

### P2-B: "Approach Grafting" `[NOW]`
Two sibling branches that grow close together can fuse into one over time — this mechanic exists nowhere else in the genre. The video shows two tips being guided toward each other, then cuts to an accelerated season view where a progress indicator grows and, at completion, a bridge node appears connecting both. The visual payoff is a branch that looks like it has always been two paths fusing into one trunk — which is exactly what approach grafting produces in real life. Ends with the fused branch isolated in close-up.

### P2-C: "Root Over Rock" `[NOW]`
Place a rock, orient the tree so its base hangs over it, advance a few seasons, and watch the roots drape down the stone face and grip it. This video is about spectacle first, mechanic second. Open with the final result — roots wrapping stone — then cut to the beginning and show the process. The Ishitsuki style is one of the most dramatic forms in real bonsai, and the game's root-draping simulation makes it look genuinely geological. No other indie game has this.

### P2-D: "The Saw Matters" `[NOW]`
Most players assume any cut is equivalent — this video shows why that is wrong. Start with a large branch that needs removing, demonstrate the wound that a small clipper leaves vs. what the saw produces, then let the seasons run and show the callus forming and slowly closing the wound. Larger tools on appropriate cuts produce cleaner healing and smaller scars. Smaller tools on large cuts leave ragged wounds that heal slowly. The consequence is visible in the mesh for the life of the tree.

### P2-E: "Soil Actually Matters" `[LATER: requires soil system]`
A tree in the wrong soil composition grows slowly, throws fewer buds per season, and eventually shows health symptoms. This video runs two identical trees in parallel — one in species-correct substrate, one in generic mix — and fast-forwards 5 years. The health divergence is visible in canopy density and vigor. Ends on the correct soil recipe for the featured species. Doubles as the in-game tutorial for the potting system.

### P2-F: "Air Layering" `[LATER: requires air layering system]`
Air layering is one of the most dramatic propagation techniques in horticulture — you're cutting a branch halfway through, wrapping the wound to encourage rooting, and eventually harvesting a new tree from the middle of a living one. This video shows the full process from initial wound to root emergence to detachment. The payoff is watching a single branch become an independent tree in the player's collection. Caption: *"Clone a branch into its own tree."*

### P2-G: "Repotting Mini-Game" `[LATER: requires repotting system]`
The repotting interaction is tactile and satisfying in a way that few bonsai game mechanics are — root raking, pot selection, fresh soil layering. This video is recorded from a close angle that emphasizes the hands-on feel of the interaction. It's the video most likely to convert players who are on the fence, because it shows that the game has texture and physicality beyond just watching a tree grow. Ends on the freshly potted tree sitting in its new container.

## Pillar 3 — "Disasters & Recoveries"

### P3-A: Fungal Cascade `[NOW]`
A healthy tree gets infected — one branch shows the first yellowing, and then the sickly tint spreads across the canopy over several seasons while health drains. The video holds on the worst moment (the tree looks genuinely diseased) before cutting to the recovery: fungicide applied, healthy seasons pass, new growth pushes through. The emotional arc is dread → relief, and the key hook is that the disease looks visually distinct from healthy foliage. Bonsai hobbyists will recognize the pathology immediately.

### P3-B: Drought Death (and Last-Minute Save) `[NOW]`
Watering stops. The health bar drains slowly at first, then faster. The foliage desaturates. Just before the death threshold, water is applied and the tree stabilizes — recovery is slow but visible over the following seasons. This video is about tension. Record it with the health UI visible so the risk reads clearly on screen. The near-death-and-recovery narrative is the most emotionally engaging format in this genre.

### P3-C: Over-Pruning Trauma `[NOW]`
A tree is aggressively trimmed in the wrong season — deep cuts, many branches removed at once. The reserve drains. Spring comes and the flush is sparse and weak compared to a healthy tree. Over the following seasons the tree slowly recovers its vigor, but the branching structure tells the story of what happened. This video targets the player behavior most likely to kill a tree by accident, and shows that mistakes are survivable but not consequence-free. Ends on a recovered tree with notably thinner branching than before the cuts.

### P3-D: Noob vs. Expert Playthrough `[LATER: requires gamification layer]`
Same species, same starting trunk, same number of seasons — two playthroughs side by side, one making optimal decisions and one making common mistakes. The gap between them after 5 simulated years shows how much the care choices matter. This is the video that will make new players feel like there is real skill to develop, and experienced players feel validated. Requires a defined set of "correct" decisions to serve as the expert line.

## Pillar 4 — "Bonsai IRL" (Educational Crossover)

Each video shows the game performing a real technique and captions it with how the technique works in real life. No sales pitch — just *"this is real, and the game simulates it."* Post the IRL crossover videos to r/bonsai with no storefront link; let the community response do the marketing.

### P4-A: Wiring — Real vs. Simulated `[NOW]`
Side-by-side of in-game wire application and a reference photo or description of copper/aluminum wire training. Caption: *"In BONSAIMANCER, wire left on too long bites into the cambium. This is exactly what happens in real life — the scar is called wire bite and it's permanent."* The game mechanic IS the real technique, so no creative license is needed.

### P4-B: Approach Grafting — Real vs. Simulated `[NOW]`
The sibling branch fusion mechanic mirrors natural approach grafting (inarchin). Caption: *"Two branches that touch long enough fuse into one. Real bonsai artists exploit this deliberately. BONSAIMANCER simulates it over 4 growing seasons."* The bonsai community will immediately recognize the technique and share it.

### P4-C: Air Layering — Real vs. Simulated `[LATER: requires air layering system]`
The in-game air layering process mirrors marcotting exactly. Caption: *"In real life this takes 4–8 weeks. In BONSAIMANCER it takes one growing season."* The detachment moment — a branch becoming its own tree — is the visual hook.

### P4-D: Seasonal Growth Windows `[NOW]`
Seasonal taper (fast spring growth, slow summer, dormancy) mirrors real fertilizer timing and growth windows that every bonsai hobbyist manages. Caption: *"Spring is when everything happens. Push a tree too hard in summer and you get weak growth. Don't fertilize in autumn — it won't store. BONSAIMANCER uses the same seasonal rules as your real tree."* This one builds credibility with the hobbyist community even before the game ships.

### P4-E: Nutrient Reserve and Fertilizer Timing `[NOW]`
The reserve system (the internal nutrient store that powers growth and healing) maps directly to how real bonsai artists manage fertilizer timing. Caption: *"Your tree has a reserve. Push it into heavy pruning with a low reserve and the recovery is brutal. Real bonsai artists call this 'the tree's energy' and build it up through the growing season before any major work."* Targets an audience that already thinks this way.

## Pillar 5 — "The Living System" (Dev Log)

### P5-A: "Procedural Bark — Zero Textures" `[NOW]`
Show the full range of bark types available in the shader — groove depth, groove scale, color tiers, wound layers — by rotating through several species and zooming into the bark surface. The hook is that there are no texture files: every pattern is generated mathematically per-pixel. Caption: *"Every bark pattern in BONSAIMANCER is generated in real-time. No textures. Just math."* This will resonate with technical audiences and shader enthusiasts.

### P5-B: "Branches That Touch Long Enough Merge" `[NOW]`
A dev-log reveal of the sibling branch fusion system. Show the proximity detection, the season counter ticking up on a pair of tips, and the moment the bridge node appears. Caption: *"I added a mechanic where branches that stay close long enough fuse together. It's approach grafting. It actually happens in real bonsai. It took 3 weeks to simulate correctly."* Transparency + mechanic depth = dev-log engagement.

### P5-C: "Trunk Geometry Pass" `[LATER: requires vertex jitter + junction swell]`
Before-and-after of the trunk mesh. The smooth cylinder becomes a living thing with bark texture variation, subtle undulation, and swelling at branch junctions. Caption: *"I was never happy with the trunk. Here's the version I've been working toward."* This is the highest-production dev-log video — hold it until the geometry feels genuinely impressive.

### P5-D: "Buds Are Alive Now" `[LATER: requires bud visuals]`
A zoomed-in look at the new bud mesh and animation system. Show bud swelling, the seasonal color shift, and bud break. Caption: *"I finally added buds. They swell before spring, then break. It took longer than I expected."* This is a detail-nerd video — small thing, big signal about the level of care in the simulation.

### P5-E: "You Can Decorate Your Tree Now" `[LATER: requires decoration system]`
Show the decoration placement UI, a range of ornament options, and how they interact with the tree mesh. Caption: *"Added decorations. I went a little overboard."* Light tone, shows personality, re-engages existing followers.

### P5-F: "Bonsai Forest" `[LATER: requires multi-tree system]`
Multiple trees in a single composition — the Yose-ue style. Show the forest from a cinematic orbit, individual trees at different stages, the visual depth of a multi-trunk grouping. This is the big reveal video for a major system addition and should be saved for maximum impact. Caption: *"You can grow a whole forest now."*
